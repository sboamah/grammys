Are the Grammy's keeping up?

By Sylvia Boamah
The Recording Academy was founded in 1957 and formed the Grammy Awards two years after to recognize "Outstanding Achievement in the music industry". The Academy and its famed award show has had moments of controversy surrounding its lack of diversity, nominee choices, and nomination process.
Below shows a comparison between the Grammys and the Billboard charts, a weekly calculation of music popularity. There's a stark difference between the two.

This begs the question: Are the Grammys for the populus or for its members?


Nomination process

To best answer this question, it's important to first understand the nomination process.
Around 350 experts in various fields out of over 12,000 voting members determine whether or not each entry is eligible and sort submissions into the 84 categories within 30 fields/genres. Expertise is determined by what credits you have music on. (i.e. If you're a producer for a rock album and an R&B album, you are considered a rock and R&B expert).
Members can vote only in their area of expertise; up to 15 of the Grammy Awards' categories, as well as in the four all-genre general field categories (Record of the Year, Album of the Year, Song of the Year, Best New Artist).


why are the Grammys and Billboard different?

According to the Hollywood Reporter, The Academy had 14,553 voting (professionals who apply to the Academy specifically to vote) and professional members in 2020. While Billboard reflects the populus by breaking down consumer dollars and music streams, the Grammy nominations are heavily related to the demographic makeup of Academy members.
